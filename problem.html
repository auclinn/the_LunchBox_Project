Food Waste In Philippines

According to the World Wide Fund Philippines (WWF PH), composting company GreenSpace, about a ton of food waste is collected every week from partner households and businesses in Metro Manila alone. The capital region generates around 2,000 tons or 2 million kilograms of food waste daily. This waste contributes significantly to greenhouse gas emissions and climate change, as rotting food produces methane gas, a potent greenhouse gas.

Larger meal portion sizes:

Households tend to prepare more food than is actually consumed, resulting in leftovers that often
end up being discarded.

Greater number of household members:

Larger families may have a harder time consuming all the prepared food, leading to more waste.

Higher wealth status:

Households with higher wealth and income levels are more likely to waste food. Wealthier families
can afford to buy more food, some of which may go unconsumed and end up being discarded.

Seeing Beyond: Global Impact of Foodwaste

The implications of food waste extend far beyond local borders, affecting the global economy, environment, and food security.

Economic Impact:

Food waste represents a massive financial loss. The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) estimates that approximately one-third of all food produced for human consumption is wasted globally each year. This amounts to a staggering economic loss of around $1 trillion annually. This waste has ripple effects, from lost income for farmers to increased prices for consumers.


Environmental Consequences:

The environmental toll of food waste is equally alarming. Food production is resource-intensive, requiring vast amounts of water, land, and energy. When food is wasted, so are these resources. Additionally, food waste in landfills decomposes anaerobically, producing methane, a potent greenhouse gas. According to the United Nations, if food waste were a country, it would be the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases after the United States and China.


Food Security and Social Impact:

With millions of people worldwide suffering from hunger and malnutrition, food waste is a stark contradiction. The World Food Programme reports that nearly 690 million people are hungry, while about 1.3 billion tons of food is wasted each year. Reducing food waste could alleviate hunger by redirecting surplus food to those in need.
